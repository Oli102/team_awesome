Sensing Loop
WATCH → INTERPRET → RESPOND
01
Watch
BlazePose (via ml5.js) runs on every webcam frame. It extracts 33 body keypoints — nose, ears, shoulders, elbows, wrists — each with an X, Y, and confidence score. Only keypoints above 0.2 confidence are trusted.
→
02
Interpret
detection.js runs four calculations per frame: shoulder-to-nose distance (posture), ear-to-nose ratio (gaze), wrist velocity delta (hands), and nose X-drift (centering). Each produces a raw signal score.

stateEngine.js integrates those signals over time into four 0–100 gauges using a decay rate of 0.98 and a fill rate of ±2 per frame. This smooths noise and prevents flickering.
→
03
Respond
response.js renders four arc gauges over the mirrored video feed. A centered status bar shows a priority message: the worst current failure gets named first. A ghost skeleton overlays the optimal body position.

Diptych Framing
PRESSURE / POSSIBILITY
← This System
PRESSURE
Presenter Coach lives on the pressure pole. It watches you constantly, scores you against a fixed behavioral ideal, and names your failures in real time. The gauge dropping is a verdict. The message bar telling you to "FIX YOUR POSTURE" is a command. The camera is not neutral — it is a standard you are expected to meet.

Being watched by this system feels like being evaluated. The ghost skeleton overlaid on your body is the shape you're supposed to be.
The Other Pole →
POSSIBILITY
The opposite system would not measure you against an ideal. It would watch for what is interesting: unexpected movement, stillness that reads as contemplation, asymmetry that feels intentional. It would not score you — it would reflect you. Sitting in front of that system would feel less like an audition and more like being seen.

This system is built on the premise that good presenting has a correct form — that posture, gaze, gesture, and centering can be measured against an optimal and found wanting. That premise is not neutral. Every threshold in config.js is a value judgment rendered as a number.

To build the opposite pole would require dismantling that premise entirely. The camera would stay; the scoring would go. What remains is a different question: not "are you performing correctly?" but "what are you actually doing, and is that worth watching?"
